NPO Website Redesign: Backpacks 4 Vic Kids

Details & Deliverables
Team Size, Role & Responsibilities

Our team consisted of 3 invaluable team mates: Andre White, Melissa Naval and myself. Each member contributed equally and generously picked up the workload for one another when required - working overtime for the same goal. There was particular synergy during the research phase, and syncopation when prototyping.

Deliverables

A formal presentation and slide deck (found below) comprising all research artefacts and a demonstration of the website prototype.

Tools Used

Google Drive, Slides, Docs, Sheets and Google Forms. Figma, Figjam, Miro, and Zoom.

Timeline & Scope

3 weeks of design time, 1 week for presentation preparation and polish


Design Process


Our team formulated a proto-persona: the sort of person who might be more aware of social causes, who may or may not be in a position to donate or volunteer, who feels unsure of how to contrbute in the best way, and generally, the sort of person who wants the best for others. This profile guided our decisions with whom we wanted to interview. Once we had 5 agreeable interviewees, we asked them questions to gain insight into what motivates them to want to help, what might help them engage with an interested NPO, to understand the rationale behind donating versus volunteering; and to enquire about the sorts of characteristics an NPO might demonstrate that makes them want to support on a longterm basis. Andre and Melissa organised a research survey, through Google Forms, which brought empiricism and qualitive measures to our research study.

Andre corroborated our user research insights with Backpacks 4 Vic Kids' organisational founder, Sally Beard, who confirmed our survey results: that most contributors learn of B4VK through word of mouth interactions with people close to the cause, and that the website is the organisation's primary source of information and generator of donations and volunteer requests. Our findings were synthesised into an Affinity Diagram, Empathy Map and a User Persona (Betty Full), Value Proposition, User Journey Map and Storyboard. These artefacts became the foundation for our ideation and iterations.

From our Value Poroposition, we identified a few key implementable improvements that would improve the site navigation and donations experiences. They were: a more organised and methodical information architecture for the site, as most found it overwhelming, content-heavy, difficult to skim and visually, a little dated. The donation options felt scattered, and users were unsure what kind of benefit their donations provided - given a multitude of options. With a Feature Prioritisation Matrix, we remedied these issues with a clear calendar where users could register their volunteering interest, and a few new "social" initiatives: "Share with friends" and "Create a Fund Drive" options - so that users could set goals and work towards them in-time, with the support of peer groups and communities.

A number of sketch pages were produced, with the aim of exploring questions around button placement, mapping, iconography, card content and chunking, and even, some heuristic development. These sketches were translated into a high-fidelity Figma mockup, which remained truthful to the user flow identified in the ideation phase. Wireframes were linked together to create an illustrative prototype, ready for user testing and feedback.

User Testing uncovered four bottlenecks for our users that impacted their ability to navigate the site intuitively. More eye-catching callouts were rearranged on the homepage, according to hierarchical importance for users. The volunteer calendar was redesigned to include a key so that users could feel more confident with their selections. Lastly, an uppermost search bar was removed as our redesigned site's pages had been re-consolidated under dropdown menus and user testing proved them easy to navigate to find what they were searching for.
